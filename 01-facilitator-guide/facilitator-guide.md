**Author:** Jelani D. Maitland

## 1. What Is a Tabletop Exercise?

A tabletop exercise (TTX) is a discussion-based walkthrough of a simulated incident scenario. Participants talk through how they would respond to an unfolding situation, step by step. No systems are touched. No emails are actually sent. The goal is to test decision-making, communication, and coordination, not technical skills.

Think of it as a fire drill for cybersecurity, except instead of evacuating a building, you are walking through "what would we actually do if this happened right now?"

For MedCaribe, tabletop exercises test whether the Incident Response Plan, Escalation Decision Tree, and supporting policies created in the security program actually work when people are under pressure and making real-time decisions.

---

## 2. Why Run Tabletop Exercises?

Tabletop exercises reveal gaps that documentation alone cannot uncover:

- Does the Incident Coordinator know they have pre-authorized containment authority?
- Does the receptionist know who to call if they spot something suspicious?
- Does the Medical Director understand when regulatory notification is required?
- Do staff know the difference between a security event and an incident?
- Are the phone numbers in the escalation tree actually correct and reachable?

A plan that has never been tested is a plan that will fail when it matters. Tabletop exercises are the lowest-cost, lowest-risk way to find those failures before a real incident does.

## 3. Facilitator Role

### 3.1 Who Should Facilitate?

The facilitator should be someone who is familiar with the incident response plan and the scenarios, is NOT one of the primary participants (they should observe and guide, not respond), can keep discussions on track without dominating them, and is comfortable asking probing follow-up questions.

For MedCaribe, the ideal facilitator is someone external (such as a cybersecurity advisor) or, if running internally, a person who is not assigned a role in the IR Plan. The IT Administrator should participate, not facilitate, since they hold a key response role.

### 3.2 Facilitator Responsibilities

**Before the exercise:**
- Select the scenario and review all injects and discussion prompts
- Schedule 90-120 minutes in a quiet room (no interruptions)
- Send the participant handout to all attendees at least 3 business days in advance
- Print copies of the scenario (facilitator copy only; participants should not see injects in advance)
- Print observer worksheets for any designated observers
- Confirm the IR Plan, Escalation Decision Tree, and relevant policies are available for reference during the exercise (printed or on screen)
- Prepare a brief opening statement (see Section 5)

**During the exercise:**
- Present each inject one at a time and allow discussion before moving on
- Ask probing questions when the group gets stuck or skips important steps
- Keep time and move the group forward if discussion stalls
- Do NOT provide answers or correct participants during the exercise (note gaps silently for the debrief)
- Track key observations on the Observer Worksheet

**After the exercise:**
- Lead the hot wash (immediate debrief, 15-20 minutes)
- Compile findings into the After-Action Report within 14 days
- Present findings and recommendations to leadership
- Track improvement actions to completion

## 4. Exercise Planning Checklist

Use this checklist 2-4 weeks before the exercise date:

**4 weeks before:**
- [ ] Select scenario
- [ ] Confirm date, time, and location with all participants
- [ ] Identify facilitator and observer(s)

**2 weeks before:**
- [ ] Send calendar invite with exercise overview (not the scenario details)
- [ ] Distribute participant handout
- [ ] Confirm all participants have read the IR Plan (or at minimum the Escalation Decision Tree)

**1 week before:**
- [ ] Facilitator reviews full scenario including all injects and prompts
- [ ] Print facilitator scenario copy, observer worksheets, and extra participant handouts
- [ ] Confirm room booking (whiteboard or flip chart available is helpful)
- [ ] Test any presentation equipment if using slides for injects

**Day of exercise:**
- [ ] Arrive 15 minutes early to set up
- [ ] Place participant handouts and reference documents at each seat
- [ ] Confirm attendance against the roster
- [ ] Remind participants: no phones, no email during the exercise

## 5. Running the Exercise

### 5.1 Opening (10 minutes)

Read or paraphrase the following:

"Thank you for participating in today's tabletop exercise. This is a discussion-based exercise designed to test our incident response procedures. There are no wrong answers. The goal is not to test individuals but to test our processes and identify areas for improvement.

Here are the ground rules:
- This is a safe space. Be honest about what you would actually do, not what you think the right answer is.
- There are no trick questions. I will present the scenario in stages and ask how you would respond.
- Refer to our Incident Response Plan and Escalation Decision Tree as needed. That is exactly what they are for.
- Please silence your phones and give this your full attention for the next 90 minutes.
- Everything discussed here stays in this room unless we agree otherwise.

I will present a scenario that unfolds over several stages. At each stage, I will describe what has happened and ask the group how you would respond. Take your time. Talk it through as a team.

Let's begin."

### 5.2 Inject Delivery

Each scenario contains 4-6 injects (stages of the incident). For each inject:

1. Read the inject aloud (or display on screen)
2. Pause and let participants absorb the information
3. Ask the primary discussion question for that inject
4. Let the group discuss freely for 5-10 minutes
5. Use the follow-up probing questions if the group misses key points
6. Summarize what was decided before moving to the next inject

**Timing guidance:** Allow 10-15 minutes per inject. A 5-inject scenario should take approximately 60-75 minutes of discussion, plus opening and closing.

### 5.3 Facilitator Tips

**If the group gets stuck:** Ask "Who would you call first?" or "What does the IR Plan say about this?" Redirect to the documentation rather than providing the answer.

**If one person dominates:** Say "That's a good point. [Other person], what would your role be at this stage?"

**If the group jumps ahead:** Say "Let's pause there. Before we get to recovery, what containment steps would you take first?"

**If participants argue about the right approach:** This is good. Capture both perspectives and note it as a discussion point for the debrief.

**If a participant says "I don't know":** That is a valid and valuable finding. Record it. It means a training or documentation gap exists.

### 5.4 Closing and Hot Wash (15-20 minutes)

After the final inject, lead an immediate debrief:

1. "What went well? What did we handle effectively?"
2. "Where did we get stuck or uncertain?"
3. "Were there any moments where we did not know who was responsible for a decision?"
4. "Did the IR Plan and Escalation Decision Tree help? Were they easy to use?"
5. "What is the single most important thing we should fix before the next exercise?"

Thank participants for their time and explain that a formal After-Action Report will follow within 14 days.

## 6. Post-Exercise Actions

### 6.1 After-Action Report

Complete the After-Action Report Template within 14 days. The report should include: exercise overview (date, scenario, participants), a timeline of how the group responded to each inject, strengths identified, gaps and areas for improvement, specific recommendations with owners and target dates, and updates to the risk register if new risks or changed scores are identified.

### 6.2 Improvement Tracking

Each recommendation from the AAR should be assigned an owner and a target completion date. Track these alongside the remediation roadmap from Project 1. Improvements might include updates to the IR Plan, additional training for specific roles, new or modified policies, technical control changes, or updated contact lists.

### 6.3 Frequency

MedCaribe should conduct a minimum of one tabletop exercise per year (per the IR Policy, POL-003). For maximum value, conduct two per year using different scenarios to test different aspects of the response capability.

## 7. Scenario Selection Guide

| Scenario | Best For | Tests | Recommended When |
|----------|----------|-------|-----------------|
| BEC (Scenario 1) | First exercise ever | Account compromise detection, financial fraud, email-based containment | Start here. Most realistic and common threat for MedCaribe. |
| Insider Threat (Scenario 2) | Second exercise | HR coordination, evidence preservation, DPA notification, employee-related sensitivity | After team is comfortable with basic IR processes. |
| Vendor Breach (Scenario 3) | Third exercise | Third-party dependency, limited investigation control, patient notification at scale | After vendor risk management process (POL-005) is established. |
